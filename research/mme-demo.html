Learning to Infer, Not Just Memorize, Chinese Vocabulary

Experience the phenomenon this research is about, firsthand.

You encounter a word you have never seen before. Can you figure out what it means?

“Her grandmother had always been quietly pervicacious.”

per thoroughly, to the end vic to conquer, to overcome -acious tending to

What do you think this word means?

stubbornly persistent; refusing to yield no matter what

You just inferred a word you had never seen, using nothing but its parts. Now try the same thing in a language you have never studied.

Here is a sentence in Modern Chinese. One word is left for you to infer.

在這個過程中，他獲得了很多。

“In this process, he ___ a great deal.”

獲

犭 animals, especially animals pursued, caught, controlled, or acted upon 蒦 bringing a target under control or into one's possession after it was free, dispersed, or inaccessible

得

彳 movement, conduct, or gradual progression along a course 㝵 reaching and successfully securing something, so that it becomes possessed or available

The sentence tells you he ended up with a great deal of something. What might the radicals suggest about how he came to have it?

Based on the radicals, what do you think 獲得 means?

獲得 - to obtain, to gain, to acquire. The characters together trace the full arc: pursuing and capturing something out of reach (獲), then moving toward it and securing it so it becomes yours (得).

You have probably never studied Chinese. Yet the radicals gave you enough to get there.

Native Chinese speakers use radical knowledge like this automatically. Most L2 learners never develop it. This project uses LLMs to model that ability, with the eventual goal of building radical awareness into L2 curricula.

Here is a passage from the Shānhǎijīng (Classic of Mountains and Seas), a Chinese geographical text from the 4th century BC, a challenge even for native speakers. One character has no standard translation. Infer it from its radical.

又东二百里，曰葛山，其上多赤金，其下多瑊石。

“Two hundred leagues further east is Geshan Mountain. Its top is full of red gold. Its foot is full of ___ stones.”

𤣩 𤣩 indicates the jade radical. When you see this component in a character, it often signals something related to jade, gems, or precious stones.

The passage already tells you the mountain is full of red gold at its top. What might be found at its foot?

Based on the radical, what kind of stone do you think 瑊 refers to?

瑊 - a type of precious stone or jade-like gem. The 𤣩 radical marks it immediately as something valuable and gem-like, even if you have never seen this character before.

This character appears fewer than a handful of times in all of Classical Chinese literature. Yet you just inferred its meaning.

Native Chinese speakers use radical knowledge like this automatically. Most L2 learners never develop it. This project uses LLMs to model that ability, with the eventual goal of building radical awareness into L2 curricula.
